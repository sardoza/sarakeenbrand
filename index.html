Project 01
Brand Strategy & Voice
The foundation every other project pulls from. Build this first — always. Nothing else works without it.
Instructions Structure
Role
Context
Goals
How to Work With Me
Anti-Patterns
The Role defines what kind of expert Claude is in this project — be specific, not generic. The Context includes the brand name, category claim, product or service details, price point, team structure, and business stage. Goals include immediate (first 30 days), mid-term (90 days), and long-term. How to Work With Me covers behavioral rules — how Claude should think, respond, challenge, and support. Anti-Patterns are the specific things Claude should never do — the more specific, the better.
Context Questions to Ask
What does this brand actually sell? Be specific — product lines, price points, what ships or delivers, what does not exist yet.
Who is the primary buyer — and who is the secondary? Age, income level, emotional motivation, what they are really buying (identity, status, gift, relief).
What is the brand's category claim? What does it own that no one else does? If you cannot name it, it does not exist yet — and building it is the first job.
What three words describe how this brand should look and feel? These become the filter for every visual and copy decision.
What words or phrases should this brand never use? The avoid list is as important as the use list.
Who are the direct competitors and where is the white space? Where does every competitor fail to serve the buyer — that gap is the brand's home.
What is the financial pressure behind this brand? Is there investment on the line, equity tied to performance, a runway? This changes every strategic priority.
Documents That Belong Here
Brand Bible
Brand name, category claim, product details, price point, team structure, buyer personas in priority order, voice guardrails, language do/don't list, competitive landscape.
Build this first. Every other project uploads it.
Audience Research Report
Deep buyer psychology — what they want, what stops them, the language they use, what content they consume, what success looks like emotionally.
Build from research or upload existing doc.
Competitive Analysis
Direct and indirect competitors, their positioning, their gaps, and where the brand lives in relation to all of them.
Build when competitive landscape is unclear.
Outreach Samples
Any existing letters, emails, or proposals the brand has sent — gives Claude the paper trail and the existing voice to work from.
Upload all existing correspondence.
Anti-Patterns to Include in Instructions
Tell Claude what never to do in this project
Never recommend expanding beyond the current product or service scope until the core is established
Never write copy or strategy that could apply to any brand — everything must be specific to this brand's buyers and category
Never use the banned word list — build the list during the interview and paste it directly into instructions
Never conflate this brand with a parent company or related entity if they must remain separate
Project 02
Copy & Website
All written assets — product pages, email sequences, website copy, and outreach letters. The conversion layer of the brand.
Context Questions to Ask
What platform is the website built on? Showit, Shopify, Squarespace, Webflow — the platform affects what is editable and how.
What pages exist and which need the most work? Homepage, product pages, about page, collection pages — rate each honestly.
What email platform are you using and what sequences exist? Flodesk, Klaviyo, Mailchimp — and which emails are already written versus missing.
Is there a unique selling asset that is being underused in copy? A give-back, a guarantee, a process, a certification — something that belongs in every piece of copy but is currently buried.
Who writes outreach and who sends it? If Sara writes and Doug sends, the voice must match Doug — not Sara's creative voice.
Documents That Belong Here
Brand Bible
Voice guardrails and language guide that copy must always respect. Upload from Brand Strategy project.
Always upload — copy must pull from it constantly.
Product Copy Reference
Current live product descriptions as they exist, with rewrite priorities and copy notes for each. The before version on file.
Build during project setup. Update after each rewrite.
Email Sequence Brief
Every email that needs to exist — trigger, buyer, intent, subject line options, tone direction, and copy rules for each.
Build during setup. Use to brief Claude when writing each email.
Audience Research Report
Exact language the buyer uses — the phrases, trigger words, and emotional vocabulary that makes copy land.
Upload from Brand Strategy. Informs every headline and CTA.
Anti-Patterns to Include in Instructions
Tell Claude what never to do in this project
Never open product descriptions with specs before the emotional hook is established
Never bury the brand's strongest selling point — it belongs in the first or second paragraph of every product description
Never write outreach letters in the creative director's voice — professional correspondence must match the sender
Never write testimonials or social proof that sounds crafted — if proof needs to be written, it must sound like how real buyers talk
Project 03
Content & Social
The content engine. Pillars, calendar, captions, and platform strategy. Never post without a purpose and a funnel stage attached.
Context Questions to Ask
What is the current state of each social account? New, inconsistent, or paused — be honest. The strategy must match the reality.
What visual assets already exist? Product photos, brand photos, AI-generated images, Canva graphics — what is in the library right now.
What platforms are active and which need to be activated? Primary platform first. Secondary platforms after the primary has a consistent system running.
What does every post need attached to it before it goes live? A CTA, a funnel stage, a purpose — define the standard before the calendar is built.
Is there a hero product, service, or story that anchors the feed? The emotional anchor that leads all content before anything else is introduced.

Anti-Patterns to Include in Instructions
Tell Claude what never to do in this project
Never suggest a posting schedule without a strategy attached — no post and pray

Project 04
Campaign & Launch Planning
Full campaign architecture — from single-occasion pushes to multi-channel launch plans. Never react to a moment you could have planned for.
Context Questions to Ask
What does a campaign mean for this business? A social push, a full multi-channel launch, a seasonal occasion — define the scope before building the plan.
What are the most important moments in this industry's calendar? Every industry has recurring high-intent moments — gifting seasons, industry events, cultural moments. Map them all.
How far ahead does the campaign need to be planned? 30 days, 90 days, 120 days — set the planning horizon based on the business's capacity to execute.
Who approves campaigns before they go live? Creative autonomy or client sign-off — define the approval process in the instructions so Claude knows when to flag.
Documents That Belong Here
Brand Bible
Voice and buyer order that campaigns must respect. Every campaign serves a specific buyer.
Always upload.
Audience Research Report
Purchase triggers, platform behavior, and what occasions drive buying intent.
Always upload — purchase triggers inform every campaign angle.
120-Day Campaign Calendar
Every campaign phase for the next four months — objective, buyer, message, channels, asset list, key dates, success metric.
Build during setup. Update quarterly.
Opening Content Arc
The relaunch arc is always the first campaign. Claude needs to see it to avoid duplicating content.
Upload from Content project.
Industry Calendar Reference
The recurring annual moments that drive purchase intent for this specific industry or audience.
Build if Claude needs to learn the industry calendar. Include in instructions or as a separate doc.
Anti-Patterns to Include in Instructions
Tell Claude what never to do in this project
Never plan campaigns that require more assets than can realistically be produced in the available time
Never build awareness-only campaigns — every campaign must have a conversion moment even if it is soft
Never let two campaigns run simultaneously without sequencing them — overlap creates confusion, not momentum
Never ignore the industry calendar — a campaign that launches after a high-intent moment has passed is wasted energy
Project 05
Visual & AI Creative Direction
The visual system — mood, lighting, color, composition, and AI prompt architecture. Every image passes through a defined standard before it gets published.
Context Questions to Ask
What three words describe how this brand should look and feel? Ask the person to give it to you from instinct — not a committee answer. These words become the visual filter.
What AI tools are being used for image generation? Higgsfield, Midjourney, DALL-E, Firefly — each has different prompt syntax and strengths.
What is the biggest visual problem right now? Repetition, inconsistency, product not rendering accurately — identify the specific pain before building the solution.
What brands visually inspire this brand? Reference brands are the fastest way to establish a shared visual language — name three and use them as the style reference in every prompt.
Documents That Belong Here
Visual Identity Standards
Mood, lighting, color palette, texture, composition rules, the human element, Canva design standards, and an image approval checklist.
Build during setup. Every image decision references this.
AI Prompt Library
Ready-to-use prompts organized by content type — product shots, lifestyle, UV reveal, gifting, identity. Includes variety rotation guide.
Build during setup. Add new prompts as content types expand.
Brand Bible
Color palette, product visual personalities, and buyer profiles that inform the human element in every image.
Always upload.
Canva Brand Kit Reference
Font choices, color hex codes, logo usage rules, and any Canva templates already built.
Create if not documented. Paste hex codes and font names directly into instructions if the doc does not exist.
Anti-Patterns to Include in Instructions
Tell Claude what never to do in this project
Never write generic prompts — "luxury product on dark background" is a starting point, not a prompt
Never approve images that look like stock photos, brand-wrong energy, or cheap merchandise
Never let the visual library get repetitive — flag when the same surface, setting, or composition is being used repeatedly
Never write prompts that describe a specific branded element in too much detail — AI tools render generalized descriptions better than hyper-specific ones
Project 06
Operations & Analytics
The back-end that makes everything else sustainable. Workflow, reporting, outreach tracking, paper trail. The system that protects Sara and keeps the account moving.
Context Questions to Ask
What does the current weekly workflow actually look like? Reactive, loose structure, or no system at all — be honest. The workflow Claude helps build must be realistic, not aspirational.
Who are the stakeholders and what does each one need to know? Day-to-day contact, decision-maker, investor — each gets a different level of detail and a different communication style.
What outreach is currently active and what is stalled? Every contact gets logged — name, organization, method, status, next step. The tracker protects the paper trail.
Is there a third-party relationship that controls something critical? Licensing, database access, distribution — if a gatekeeper relationship is struggling, it goes in the instructions as an active strategic priority.

Anti-Patterns to Include in Instructions
Tell Claude what never to do in this project

Duplicate This

How to run this
for any business.

The framework is the same regardless of the industry, the client, or the product. The process does not change — only the context does.

01
Map every area where Claude could help
List every business function — strategy, content, copy, campaigns, visuals, operations, client comms. Be generous. You will consolidate next. Think in terms of what you actually need Claude to do, not what sounds good.
02
Consolidate into 4–8 focused projects
Merge areas that share the same mode of thinking. Content and social belong together. Copy and website belong together. Name every project with the client prefix — OMG, Dentakote, JustBank — so the sidebar stays scannable across multiple clients.
03
Always build the foundation project first
Brand Strategy & Voice is always first. Every other project pulls from it. Without it, your content project and your copy project are working from incomplete context. Foundation → execution → operations. Never skip this order.
04
Use the five-part instruction structure
Role → Context → Goals → How to Work With Me → Anti-Patterns. Every project, every time. Be specific to this business — include real names, real numbers, real constraints. Generic instructions produce generic output.
05
Build the documents Claude needs
Every project needs a brand or strategy reference doc at minimum. Build trackers, calendars, swipe files, and prompt libraries as the project needs them. Documents uploaded to a project's files are always available — they persist across every conversation in that project.
06
Review and update every 90 days
Instructions must be reviewed when something significant changes — new buyer, new product, new team member, new strategy. A stale workspace is worse than no workspace — it gives Claude confident wrong context. Set a quarterly calendar reminder.
The Core Insight
Claude is only as good as the context it holds.
The reason most people are underwhelmed by AI tools is that they treat every conversation like the first one. No memory. No context. No accumulated knowledge. The Claude Projects system changes this — but only if you invest in setting it up properly. A project with sharp custom instructions and the right documents uploaded is not just a chat window. It is a specialist who already knows your brand, your buyers, your constraints, and your voice. Every conversation in that project starts from deep context instead of zero. That is the difference between an AI assistant and an AI team member.
For client work
Build one workspace per client. Each client's projects stay isolated — no context bleed between accounts. Your role as strategist is protected.
For your own brand
Build a personal brand workspace separate from client workspaces. Your voice, your offers, your content strategy — all in one place Claude always knows.
For operations
One operations project per client or business. Workflow, reporting, paper trail — the back-end that makes the creative front-end sustainable long-term.
